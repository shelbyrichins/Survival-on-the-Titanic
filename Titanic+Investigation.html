I also notice that Ticket may be used to identify which passengers were traveling together: there are multiple passengers on the same ticket. This raises the question:

Is Fare per person or per ticket?¶

The Data Description on kaggle indicates fare is the "Passenger fare", so I assume Fare is per passenger, not per ticket. This makes sense, as the long decimal places on some of the fares suggest a larger number was divided. It would be odd if the sale price of a ticket was actually measured to the nearest ten-thousandth.

What factors made people more likely to survive?¶

Hypotheses:¶

  • Pclass: 1st class passengers had the highest survival, followed by 2nd class passengers
  • Fare: Passengers who paid greater fares were more likely to survive
  • Sex: Female passengers were more likely to survive than male passengers
  • Age: Passengers under 18 years of age were more likely to survive
  • Ticket: Passengers were more likely to survive if they were traveling in groups
  • SibSp and Parch: Passengers were more likely to survive if they were traveling with family

Conclusions¶

Let's revisit the original question and hypotheses:

What factors made people more likely to survive?¶

I found evidence to support all of the original hypotheses:¶

Pclass:¶

1st class passengers had the highest survival, followed by 2nd class passengers.¶

63% of first class passengers survived, compared to 47% of 2nd class passengers and 24% of 3rd class passengers.

Fare:¶

Passengers who paid greater fares were more likely to survive.¶

Fare was closely associated with passenger class.

Sex:¶

Female passengers were more likely to survive than male passengers.¶

Female survial was prioritized, especially 1st and 2nd class females. In the top two classes, over 90% of females survived, while male survival was less than 40%. Only 50% of female 3rd class passengers survived, but that rate is still higher than survival of 3rd class males (14%).

Age:¶

Passengers under 18 years of age were more likely to survive.¶

Survival of children was 60%, compared with 38% for adults. Sex and passenger class also affected the survival of children, but to a lesser extend than adults.

Ticket:¶

Passengers were more likely to survive if they were traveling in groups.¶

The highest percent survival occurred for passengers on tickets with 2-3 other people.

SibSp and Parch:¶

Passengers were more likely to survive if they were traveling with their family.¶

The same pattern was found for the number of close family members aboard as for number of people on each ticket. Females generally had high survival regardless of the number of family members aboard. Males traveling alone had low survival (16%), while males traveling with 2-3 family members had 40-50% survival.
